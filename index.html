About

I am an incoming graduate student at Columbia University in the QMSS program, where I plan to continue developing my interests in economics, quantitative social science, and formal modeling. I am also a research assistant and graduating senior at Miami University, where I study political science, philosophy, and economics. My undergraduate work has brought together substantive questions about institutions, behavior, and coordination with increasingly technical interests in econometrics, computational modeling, and mathematical approaches to strategic interaction.

Across both my undergraduate research and independent projects, I am especially interested in the “micro-to-macro” problem: how individual behavioral tendencies, informational frictions, and strategic incentives aggregate into complex outcomes in markets and political systems. That interest has led me toward work in game theory, mean field games, market microstructure, applied econometrics, and related areas of quantitative social science.

More broadly, I enjoy building modular research workflows that translate messy or unstructured information into reproducible analysis. Whether working on archival metadata pipelines, NLP classification, spatial economic analysis, or independent work in mathematical finance and econometrics, I am most motivated by projects that combine substantive curiosity with rigorous empirical or formal tools.

I am especially interested in the research possibilities opened up by AI tools. Used carefully, they can compress tedious workflows, accelerate iteration, and make it easier to explore new methods, literatures, and data sources. In my own work, I have used AI-assisted tools to support coding, debugging, revision, and research discovery while keeping the substantive framing and responsibility for the work my own.
